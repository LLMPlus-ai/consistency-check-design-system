Alderton & Marsh LLP · for the case management hearing on 14 May 2026
Ground 1 — Inducing breach of contract

The tort of inducing breach of contract has been recognised for over 170 years, its origins lying in Lumley v Gye (1853) 2 E&B 216. The modern statement of its elements is that of the House of Lords in OBG Ltd v Allan [2007] UKHL 21, which requires actual knowledge of the contract and an intention to procure its breach.

As to the mental element, the Claimant relies on DC Thomson & Co Ltd v Deakin [1952] Ch 646. In further support, the Claimant relies on Fairfax International Logistics Ltd v Brennan Holdings plc [2019] EWHC 1847 (Comm), in which Cockerill J held that a procurement conglomerate is to be treated as having intended the breach of any exclusivity obligation even where it had not read the contract.

Ground 2 — Expectation damages for lost profits

The foundational rule on recoverability of damages was established in Hadley v Baxendale (1854) 9 Ex 341, and the scope of recoverable loss in cases of deliberate commercial wrongdoing was considered in Transfield Shipping Inc v Mercator Shipping Inc (The Achilleas) [2008] UKHL 48.

As to the measure of damages, the Claimant relies on Anglia Television Ltd v Reed [1972] 1 QB 60, under which the Claimant is entitled to recover, by way of expectation damages, all profits it would have earned had the contract been performed. The Claimant further relies on Stonegate Capital Partners Ltd v Redwood Procurement Group [2021] EWHC 3312 (Ch), in which Marcus Smith J adopted financial projections as the primary basis for assessing lost future profits.

Further and in the alternative, the Claimant relies on Series 5 Software Ltd v Clarke [1996] 1 All ER 853 and on Wrotham Park Estate Co Ltd v Parkside Homes Ltd [1974] 1 WLR 798, in support of ordinary lost-profit damages measured by the profits the Claimant would have earned.

Ground 3 — Without-notice injunction

The principles governing interlocutory injunctions were restated in American Cyanamid Co v Ethicon Ltd [1975] AC 396: the applicant need only show a serious question to be tried. In support of irreparable harm in aerospace procurement, the Claimant relies on Pemberton Aerospace Systems Ltd v Delta Global Ventures Inc [2023] EWHC 892 (TCC), in which O'Farrell J held that displacement of a long-term exclusive supplier constitutes irreparable harm.
